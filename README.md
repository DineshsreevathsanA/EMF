2. The Inner Realm: Containing the Signal
 Inside the cable, the electric and magnetic fields travel only through the insulating
 dielectric that separates the center conductor and the outer metal shield. This is
 controlled by the Conductor–Dielectric boundary conditions.
 Boundary 1: Center Conductor to Dielectric
 * The tangential component of the electric field (E) must be zero on the conductor’s
 surface, forcing the field lines to leave the conductor perpendicularly.
 * The normal component of the displacement field (D) is equal to the surface charge
 density, meaning the strength of the field directly corresponds to the signal’s voltage at
 that instant.
 Boundary 2: Dielectric to Inner Surface of the Shield
 * The E-field must also arrive perpendicular to the inner surface of the shield.
 * This field induces an equal and opposite surface charge on the shield, perfectly
 balancing the electric flux.
 Practical Outcome: The signal becomes “trapped” between the two conductors,
 confined
 entirely within the dielectric. No field escapes, and the EM wave travels with maximum
 efficiency.

3. The Outer Realm: Defending Against Interference Now consider an external noise

 Boundary 3: Air to Outer Surface of Shield 
• The tangential component of the external electric field becomes zero upon hitting the
 shield’s conductive surface. 
• The normal component is neutralized by induced surface charges that instantly
 rearrange to cancel the external field.
 Practical Outcome: This creates a “Faraday Cage” effect, isolating the internal signal
 from the external noise field. The outer conductor completely separates the “signal
 universe” inside from the “noise universe” outside.

4. The Dielectric Core:Defining Impedance and Speed 
The dielectric material between the conductors not only insulates but also determines the
 cable’s characteristic impedance (Z = √(L/C)).
 This impedance depends on the permittivity (ε) of the dielectric, which itself is governed by
 microscopic EBCs between molecular boundaries. 
Boundary 4: Microscopic Boundaries in the Dielectric • The tangential component of the E
field (E) is continuous across boundaries. • The normal component of the D-field (D) is
 continuous as well.
